Part One: Introduction and Methodological Tools

---

Chapter 1: Introduction – What is Black Magic?

Black magic is one of the most complex and charged concepts in the social sciences and anthropology. Is it an actual practice? A superstition? Or perhaps a social accusation reflecting tensions and conflicts within communities? In this study, we will examine the phenomenon from a scientific perspective, using anthropological, psychological, and historical tools.

Anthropologist Laurent Dousset notes that "magic and 'black' are far from being limited to the non-Western world. In the industrialized world as well, there is a renewed awakening in beliefs and practices related to the supernatural world." This phenomenon does not belong only to the "primitive other," but is an integral part of the universal human experience.

1.1 Definitions and Boundaries

Throughout this study, we will use the following definition: Black magic is the use of ritualistic, symbolic, or material techniques, with the declared intention of negatively affecting another person, their property, or their situation, while activating supernatural or energetic forces. This definition is based on Wintrob's 1973 research, describing harmful magic as a belief system attributed to malicious intentions, envy, and resentment.

It is important to distinguish between:

· Actual black magic: Rituals that people actually perform
· Accusations of black magic: Attributing malicious intentions to others
· Literary and cultural representations: Images created in literature, cinema, and popular culture

1.2 The Universal Dimension versus Cultural Specificity

Anthropology has long grappled with the dichotomy between universalism and cultural relativism. Is black magic a universal human phenomenon? Or is each expression of it unique to a particular society and understandable only in its context?

Dousset offers an approach combining both perspectives: "The first perspective integrates these beliefs and practices into the local historical, material, and social contexts of their appearance and survival. The second perspective recognizes the place of magic among all those phenomena that seem to be human by nature and suggest universalism."

This study adopts an integrated approach: we will identify universal patterns recurring in different cultures, while examining the unique ways they are expressed in each cultural context.

1.3 The Methodological Challenge

Studying black magic presents unique methodological challenges. How does one study a phenomenon defined as secretive, dangerous, and often invisible? Nina Izel, a researcher in the field, notes that "in most cases, individuals are unaware that they are victims." Low self-awareness among participants, fears of stigma, and blurring between reality and imagination—all these complicate research.

In this study, we will use the following sources:

1. Classic ethnographies: Field studies by anthropologists in various societies
2. Historical studies: Documentation of practices throughout history
3. Psychiatric and psychological studies: Examining the mental effects of beliefs in magic
4. Analysis of ritual literature: Critical examination of spell and ritual books

Chapter 2: Disclaimer and Psychological Warning

2.1 Warning to the Reader

Important reading before using this book

This book is intended for academic, anthropological, and historical research purposes only. It is not intended to serve as a practical guide for performing any rituals, spells, or magical practices. The information presented here is based on academic, ethnographic, and historical sources, and is meant to illuminate a complex human cultural phenomenon.

Serious warning: Attempting to perform the rituals described in this book may cause severe harm, including:

1. Psychological harm: Damage to mental health, development of anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorders, and psychosis
2. Physical harm: Use of dangerous substances, self-harm, health risks
3. Social harm: Social ostracism, criminal accusations, harm to family and community
4. Legal harm: Many practices described here constitute criminal offenses in most countries of the world

2.2 The Psychology Behind Belief in Black Magic

Anisha Jaiswal, a clinical psychologist, explains that "the psychology behind beliefs in black magic is fascinating. Often, people attribute unexplained events or bad luck to black magic. What drives these beliefs?"

Psychological research offers several explanations:

2.2.1 Confirmation Bias

Humans tend to seek information that confirms their existing beliefs and ignore contradictory information. If a person believes they are a victim of black magic, they will focus on every negative event in their life as "proof," while ignoring successes and neutral events.

2.2.2 Suggestion and Manipulation

Suggestions from others can deeply influence our beliefs. Spiritual leaders, shamans, or purported "experts" can convince people they are victims of magical attack, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.

2.2.3 Cognitive Dissonance

When we hold contradictory beliefs, we experience mental discomfort. Belief in a rational world alongside experiences of unexplained suffering creates dissonance. Belief in black magic resolves the dissonance: "This isn't an unfair world—someone is intentionally harming me."

2.2.4 Fear and Anxiety

Fear drives us to seek explanations and solutions. In a world full of uncertainty, belief in black magic offers a simple, clear explanation for suffering. Moreover, it also offers a solution: protection rituals, curse removal, and appeals to healers.

2.2.5 Mass Hysteria

Collective beliefs can spread rapidly through a group. Outbreaks of "magic attacks" in villages and communities demonstrate how panic can spread, creating real physical and psychological symptoms in many.

2.2.6 The Nocebo Effect

The nocebo effect is a phenomenon where negative beliefs create real negative outcomes. A person convinced they are a victim of a curse may develop real physical and psychological symptoms, as a direct result of the belief. Nina Izel describes these symptoms: "Persistent heavy and dark energy, increasing self-doubt and thought loops, negative voices pushing you toward madness, a strong feeling that something is wrong, fear or anxiety, constant fatigue, falling into despair, hallucinations, insomnia, or loss of appetite."

2.2.7 Psychological Need for Control

Beliefs provide a sense of control in the face of uncertainty. When life is chaotic, belief in black magic offers order: there is a reason for suffering, and there is a way to act against it. The sense of control, even if illusory, may reduce anxiety and provide comfort.

2.3 Recommendations for Safe Reading

To read this book safely and beneficially:

1. Maintain critical thinking: Examine every claim in light of scientific knowledge and logic
2. Recognize cognitive biases: Be aware of your natural tendencies to seek confirmation
3. Consult professionals: If you experience concerning symptoms, consult a psychologist or psychiatrist
4. Do not experiment: Do not attempt to perform rituals or spells—the potential harm outweighs any benefit
5. Separate description from action: Anthropological description of a phenomenon is not a recommendation to adopt it

Rituals 1-10: European Traditions

[1] The Black Mass

The Black Mass is a parody ritual of the Catholic Mass, attributed to satanic worship. Its historical origins are shrouded in mystery, and the most famous descriptions come from the writings of Jules Michelet and the Inquisition. In fact, there is no reliable historical documentation of a Black Mass before the 17th century. The ritual is described as including the use of an inverted altar, reciting prayers backwards, and using a consecrated chalice and an inverted cross. In the 19th and 20th centuries, the ritual was adopted by various occult groups, but most descriptions are products of literary imagination rather than documentation of authentic practice.

[2] Necromancy

Necromancy is communication with the dead for the purpose of obtaining information, usually about the future. The name originates from Greek: nekros (dead) and manteia (divination). The practice was common in ancient Greece and Rome, and in the Middle Ages was considered a serious form of witchcraft. Rituals often included visiting graves, using blood sacrifices, and calling the names of the dead. Psychologically, necromancy reflects the universal human need to confront death and seek knowledge beyond the boundaries of life.

[3] Voodoo Dolls

Contrary to popular imagery, Voodoo dolls are not central to the original Haitian Voodoo tradition. The image of a doll with pins actually comes from European traditions (poppets) and popular culture. The ritual involves creating a figure representing the victim, and then performing actions on the doll meant to affect the victim—sticking pins, tying, burning. This is a classic expression of sympathetic magic, based on the law of similarity. In the European tradition, the poppet was also used for healing—the witch "healed" the doll to heal the patient.

[4] Witch Bottles

Witch bottles are an English practice from the 17th century, where a glass or pottery bottle containing sharp objects (nails, pins) along with the victim's urine or hair was buried. The purpose was to protect against witches or return a curse to its sender. Many bottles have been excavated archaeologically, indicating the prevalence of the practice. Anthropologically, this is an expression of the law of contagion—materials from the human body maintain a connection to it. The bottle was buried or placed in the hearth, and the heat would cause it to explode—the belief was that the explosion would harm the witch.

[5] Greek Curse Tablets (Greek Katadesmoi/Defixiones)

Curse tablets (defixiones) were a common practice in the Greco-Roman world. On thin lead tablets, they would write the victim's name and the curse, sometimes using inverted letters or convoluted language, then fold, pierce with a nail, and deposit in sacred places, graves, or wells. Thousands of such tablets have been found, documenting curses for various purposes: legal (to harm an opponent in court), athletic (to harm competitors), romantic (to bind a woman to a man), and economic (to harm business competitors). The ritual reflects a perception of writing as possessing magical power.

[6] Norse Curses (Norse Nithing Pole)

The nithing pole was a Viking practice of public cursing. A nithing was a person without honor, and the pole was meant to humiliate the victim and bring them bad luck. The ritual involved erecting a pole with a horse's head, bearing runic curses, in front of the enemy's house. The curse was accompanied by words: "May you be a nithing, just as you bring disaster upon us." This is an expression of public magic, connected to honor and social shame. Sometimes the pole was placed so the horse's head pointed toward the enemy, and the curse was intended to bring disaster, famine, or death upon them.

[7] Demonic Invocation

In the Christian tradition, summoning demons was considered a grave sin. Grimoires (spell books) from the Middle Ages, such as the "Key of Solomon" and the "Black Pullet," contained detailed instructions for summoning demons to gain power, wealth, or knowledge. The rituals included drawing protective circles, using sacred names, and making sacrifices. Historically, most of these grimoires are late literary products, not documentation of authentic practice. They reflected more the fantasies of inquisitors than the actual deeds of witches.

[8] Hellfire Club Rituals

Hellfire Club was a general name for several 18th-century British aristocratic clubs, notorious for their alleged debauchery and blasphemy. The most famous was founded by Sir Francis Dashwood. Members met at Medmenham Abbey and held rituals rumored to include devil worship and black masses. In reality, these were likely provocations by wealthy young men rather than authentic satanic worship. The club was intended more to shock conservative society than to actually worship Satan.

[9] Scottish Curses (Scottish Sianachd)

Sianachd is an ancient Scottish curse tradition. The witch would curse using bones, stones, or personal belongings of the victim. The curse was accompanied by recitations in ancient Gaelic. The belief was that the curse could cause illness, death, or crop failure. The tradition reflects the importance of honor and revenge in traditional Scottish society. The curse was particularly effective if performed in a sacred place or using the victim's personal belongings.

[10] Welsh Hounds of Annwn

Annwn is the Otherworld in Welsh mythology, equivalent to the underworld. The Hounds of Annwn are white ghost dogs with red ears, who hunt the souls of the dead. Certain rituals were intended to summon these dogs against enemies. The believer would go out at night, call the names of the dogs, and ask them to take their enemies. This is an expression of magic connected to the world of the dead and wild natural forces. The belief was that the dogs would not rest until they found the victim and took them to the Otherworld.

Rituals 11-22: Continuation of European Traditions

[11] Cornish Pike of Penwith

In Cornwall, southwest England, there is a tradition of using an iron or wooden staff to perform curses. The staff, called a "pike," was buried in the ground with its tip pointing toward the victim's house. The belief was that the staff would draw negative energies and direct them toward the victim. The practice was sometimes combined with reciting curses in ancient Cornish Gaelic. The staff also served as a protective charm—when the tip pointed outward, it protected the house from evil spirits.

[12] Osculum Infame

Osculum Infame, the "shameful kiss," is a ritual of which witches in medieval Europe were accused. According to Inquisition writings, the witch would kiss the anus of Satan as a sign of absolute submission. It is important to emphasize: there is no reliable documentation of this ritual outside the indictments of the Inquisition. Most scholars see this as a product of the imagination of witch-hunters, who sought to portray witches as sexually deviant and absolute heretics.

[13] Transvection

Transvection is the belief in the ability of witches to fly through the air, usually on a broomstick or animal. The belief was widespread in medieval Europe and also appeared in the mythologies of other cultures. Its origin likely lies in the use of witch ointments containing psychoactive plants, which caused sensations of flying and hallucinations. Witches "flew" to the meeting place (the sabbath) after applying the ointment to their skin. Psychologically, this is an experience of liberation and boundary-crossing.

[14] Summoning of the Four Crown Princes of Hell

In Christian demonology, the four crown princes of hell are Satan, Lucifer, Belial, and Leviathan. Summoning rituals were intended to invoke these entities to gain power, wealth, or knowledge. The rituals included drawing protective circles, using sacred names, and making sacrifices. Various grimoires describe different hierarchies of demons, and the rituals vary accordingly. It is important to note that most of these rituals are literary products rather than documentation of authentic practice.

[15] Ritual of the Infernal Names

The ritual of reciting infernal names involves chanting demonic names from traditional lists, such as those appearing in the "Key of Solomon" or the "Lemegeton." The belief is that each name has a special power, and reciting it at the right time and place can invoke the entity. The ritual requires precise knowledge and intense concentration, as a mistake in recitation could endanger the summoner. Psychologically, the repetition of names creates a special state of consciousness and reinforces belief in their power.

[16] The Munich Manual Rituals

The "Munich Manual" is one of the most famous medieval grimoires, containing detailed instructions for summoning demons, performing curses, and creating amulets. The rituals described include using special plants, appropriate hours, and precise recitation of names. The manual reflects the medieval worldview, where magic was perceived as an exact science, not a superstition. It is important to note that the manual was written by churchmen, not by witches, and therefore reflects more the church's perception of magic than authentic practice.

[17] The Bond of Solomon

The Bond of Solomon is a ritual for making a pact with demons, attributed to the biblical King Solomon. According to legend, Solomon made a pact with demons to help him build the Temple. The ritual includes signing in blood on parchment, offering a sacrifice, and reciting oaths. The demon undertakes to serve the magician for a certain period, in exchange for their soul or other services. This is a Christian version of more ancient blood pacts, and it reflects the dualistic worldview of Christianity—a world of good and evil, God and Satan.

[18] Knights Templar Rituals

The Knights Templar were accused by King Philip IV of France of heresy, idol worship, and secret rituals including spitting on the cross, denying Jesus, and worshiping a mysterious head. Most scholars see these accusations as a political plot designed to eliminate the wealthy order and seize its property. However, the Templars may indeed have had secret initiation rituals, containing symbolic elements that were misunderstood by their investigators. The story demonstrates how accusations of black magic often serve as a political tool.

[19] Simon Magus's Flight

Simon Magus is a figure from the 1st century CE, considered by the Church Fathers to be the father of heresy and witchcraft. According to legend, he attempted to fly through the air before Emperor Nero, but fell to earth due to the prayers of Saint Peter. The legend symbolizes the struggle between Christianity and witchcraft, between true faith and false magic. Simon Magus became the archetype of the magician-sorcerer, and his story was used to justify the persecution of witches.

[20] Ritual of the Scapegoat

The scapegoat ritual originates in the Bible (Leviticus 16), where on Yom Kippur the High Priest would place the sins of the people upon a goat sent into the wilderness. In black magic, the ritual was adopted as a ceremony for placing guilt and curse upon a person or animal, who were then expelled from the community, carrying the evil with them. This is a practice of transferring impurity—the evil leaves the community attached to its carrier. The ritual reflects the human need for purification and removal of guilt.

[21] Finnish Curses (Finnish Sampo Curse)

In Finnish mythology, the Sampo is a magical object that brings wealth and good fortune. The Sampo curse is a ritual where the witch curses their rival using symbols of the Sampo. The curse was intended to cause loss of wealth, fortune, and power. Finnish tradition is rich in rune-songs, where the words themselves carry magical power. The Finnish witch would sing or chant the curse, and the words would create reality.

[22] Sami Drum Curses (Sami Noaidi Drum Curse)

The Sami (Lapps) are an indigenous people in northern Scandinavia. The Noaidi is the Sami shaman, who uses a ritual drum to communicate with the spirit world. The drum has various symbols, and the Noaidi strikes it to enter a trance. In certain cases, the Noaidi could use the drum to harm enemies—directing evil spirits toward them. The practice reflects the shamanic worldview, where everything is connected, and the shaman can navigate the forces.

Rituals 23-33: African and Afro-Caribbean Traditions

[23] Haitian Voodoo

Voodoo is a religion originating from the encounter between West African traditions (mainly Fon and Yoruba) and Roman Catholicism in Haiti. It includes worship of loa (spirits), communication with ancestors, and healing rituals. The negative image of Voodoo in the West stems from colonial stereotypes and Hollywood films. In reality, Voodoo is a complex religion with rich theology, morality, and community. Harmful magic (sometimes called the "left-hand path") exists, but it is marginal relative to general religious practice.

[24] Bokor

A bokor is a term for a Voodoo priest specializing in practical, sometimes harmful, magic. The bokor works with death spirits (especially Baron Samedi) and uses bones and graveyard earth. They can prepare amulets, cast curses, and even create zombies. It is important to understand that the bokor is not necessarily "evil"—they provide services to the community, including protection and healing. The image of the bokor as an evil sorcerer is a Western stereotype.

[25] Voodoo Doll

Contrary to popular imagery, dolls with pins are not a central practice in Haitian Voodoo. The image likely comes from European traditions (poppets) and popular culture. However, ritual dolls do exist in Voodoo, but they are primarily used for worship of the loa, not for harming enemies. The doll represents the loa and serves as a dwelling place for the spirit. The emphasis is on respect and worship, not on harm.

[26] Zombification

The zombie in Haitian folklore is a person who supposedly died but was brought back to life by a bokor to serve as a mindless slave. The process, according to belief, involves using a special poison (zombie powder) that causes paralysis and apparent death, then "revival" using an antidote. Ethnobotanist Wade Davis researched the phenomenon and claimed to have found real poisons used in the process. However, the phenomenon is extremely rare and reflects social fears more than reality.

[27] Palo Mayombe

Palo Mayombe is a religious tradition originating in Congo, which developed in Cuba and other Latin American countries. It includes worship of nature spirits and the dead, using human bones (usually from the deceased) in building nkisi (power objects). The tradition has historically been perceived as violent and dangerous due to the use of human bones, but it is actually a legitimate religion with a complex moral system. It is important to emphasize: Palo Mayombe does not include human sacrifice, contrary to racist stereotypes.

[28] Palo Mayombe Blood Oath

In Palo Mayombe, making a pact with the spirits sometimes involves the use of blood (usually from animals). The blood "feeds" the nkisi and strengthens the connection between the believer and the spirit. The blood oath is a serious commitment, and breaking it could bring disaster. The ritual reflects the perception of blood as the carrier of life, and the idea that connection with spirits requires continuous nourishment and commitment.

[29] Nkisi

An nkisi is a sacred object in the Congolese tradition, serving as a dwelling place for a spirit. The nkisi is made of wood, horn, or other material, and contains magical substances (herbs, bones, earth). Sometimes nails are added, each nail representing a request, oath, or curse. The nkisi is used for healing, protection, maintaining oaths, and sometimes for harming enemies. It is a complex object, combining art, religion, and magic.

[30] Bocio

Bocio is a power figure from the tradition of the Fon people in Benin (West Africa). The figures are made of wood and contain magical substances. They serve various purposes: protection, healing, harming enemies, and communicating with ancestors. Bocio are sometimes adorned with ropes, feathers, and other objects, each detail carrying symbolic meaning. This is a rich and complex magical art.

[31] Santería

Santería is a religion originating in Cuba, based on Yoruba traditions (Nigeria) that blended with Roman Catholicism. The orishas (spirits) are identified with Catholic saints, and worship includes music, dance, and animal sacrifice. Magic in Santería is primarily used for healing, protection, and improving life. However, harmful rituals also exist, sometimes attributed to the "left-hand path." As with Voodoo, the negative image of Santería stems from ignorance and stereotypes.

[32] Gu Poison

Gu is a concept in Chinese magic referring to a magical poison produced by placing venomous animals (snake, scorpion, centipede, lizard) in a sealed container until they devour each other. The remaining liquid is considered a powerful poison, which can be used to harm enemies. The belief was that people could be poisoned from afar using gu, or that it could be used to control others. The practice reflects the perception of power as something that can be concentrated and directed.

[33] Nigerian Magun

Magun is a magical practice in Nigeria, intended to punish adulterers. According to tradition, the magician places a magical "thunderbolt" on a woman, which will harm any man who has relations with her. The man will suffer severe pain, temporary impotence, or death. The practice reflects the importance of sexual fidelity in traditional society, and the use of magic as a means of social control.

Rituals 34-41: Continuation of African and Afro-Caribbean Traditions

[34] San Trance Death Curse

The San people (Bushmen) of southern Africa use trance rituals for healing and communicating with spirits. In rare cases, the shaman can direct lethal energy toward an enemy while entering a deep trance. The curse operates, according to belief, through healing power directed harmfully. This is an example of the duality of shamanic power—the same power that heals can also harm.

[35] Zulu Umthakathi

In Zulu tradition, an umthakathi is a witch using evil powers to harm the community. Accusation of being an umthakathi is serious, sometimes leading to persecution and even killing. The belief is that the umthakathi can cause illness, death, crop failure, and other disasters. The witch is sometimes identified by a sangoma (healer) in divination rituals. This is an example of how black magic serves as an explanation for disaster and a mechanism of social control.

[36] Swazi Nyanga Curse

In Swaziland (Eswatini), the inyanga is a traditional healer, knowledgeable in medicinal plants and magic. As elsewhere, the inyanga can serve both healing and harm. A nyanga curse includes the use of poisonous plants, amulets, and spells. The distinction between "white nyanga" and "black nyanga" depends on the healer's intention and the nature of their actions.

[37] Yoruba Aje Witchcraft

In Yoruba tradition (Nigeria), aje is a complex concept referring to feminine power, sometimes negative. The aje are women with supernatural powers, capable of both healing and harm. They are identified with ancestors and natural forces. While the Western image of "witch" is negative, in Yoruba tradition the aje is a complex figure, potentially beneficial or harmful depending on context. This is an example of how Western perceptions of "witchcraft" do not necessarily align with other perceptions.

[38] Fon Bocio Binding

In Fon tradition (Benin), binding rituals using bocio are intended to "bind" an enemy or evil force. The figure is wrapped with ropes, fibers, or other objects, thereby "binding" the enemy's ability to harm. This is a practice of protection through symbolic binding. The figure itself becomes a dwelling place for protective power, and the binding represents the magician's control over evil forces.

[39] Quimbanda

Quimbanda is a Brazilian religious-magical tradition, connected to worship of "dark" spirits such as Exu and Pombagira. It is sometimes perceived as the "black" branch of Macumba (Umbanda). Quimbanda includes rituals for obtaining power, money, love, and sometimes for harming enemies. The spirits in Quimbanda are not necessarily "evil," but operate outside social conventions, assisting those who need help that society does not provide.

[40] Candomblé

Candomblé is a Brazilian religion originating in Yoruba, Fon, and Bantu traditions. It includes worship of orishas, dance rituals, and animal sacrifice. Magic in Candomblé is primarily used for healing and improving life. However, "black magic" (Quimbanda) exists separately from traditional Candomblé. It is important to distinguish between the religion itself and stereotypes about it.

[41] Eshú

Eshú is an important orisha in Yoruba traditions and in Santería and Candomblé. He is the messenger of the gods, guardian of crossroads, and opener of ways. Eshú is not a "devil" or "demon," as sometimes translated by missionaries, but a complex entity, mischievous and cunning, who can both help and hinder. In every ritual, one first turns to Eshú to open the way to the other gods. The image of Eshú as a devil is the result of mistaken Christian interpretation.

---

Rituals 42-52: Middle Eastern and Mediterranean Traditions

[42] Ancient Egyptian Execration Rituals

In ancient Egypt, execration rituals involved writing the names of enemies (personal or national) on pottery vessels, wax figurines, or other materials, and then breaking, burning, or burying them. The belief was that destroying the name would cause the destruction of the enemy. These rituals were performed by pharaohs and priests to protect Egypt from external enemies. This is an example of state magic, where magical power serves to protect the entire nation.

[43] Moloch Sacrifice

Moloch is a god mentioned in the Bible, to whom children were allegedly sacrificed by fire. According to accounts, the Phoenicians and Canaanites sacrificed their children to Moloch in the tophet (sacred place). Archaeology at the "tophet" site in Carthage has revealed large quantities of child and animal bones, alongside inscriptions requesting the favor of the gods. Modern scholarship is divided on whether this indeed represents systematic child sacrifice, or whether it is the burial of children who died naturally. The story reflects how biblical and Greco-Roman literature used accusations of child sacrifice to defame their enemies.

[44] Babylonian Magic (Babylonian Ušburruda)

Ušburruda is a Babylonian term for spell removal rituals. The Babylonians believed in the power of spells to harm, and therefore developed a complex system of counter-rituals. The rituals included the use of special plants, figurines, and incantations. Babylonian priests were experts in diagnosing the type of spell and selecting the appropriate counter-ritual. This is an example of how black magic and white magic are two sides of the same belief system.

[45] Sumerian Nam-Búr-Bi

Nam-búr-bi is a Sumerian term for counter-spell rituals, parallel to the Babylonian ušburruda. The Sumerians believed that illnesses and disasters were often caused by enemy spells. The nam-búr-bi rituals were intended to "undo" the spell and return it to the sender. The rituals included the use of holy water, amulets, and incantations recited by priests. This is evidence of the antiquity of belief in harmful magic.

[46] Hittite Substitute King Ritual

In the Hittite kingdom (Anatolia, 13th century BCE), a complex ritual existed where, when evil omens predicted the king's death, a substitute king was crowned for a short period. The substitute king bore the curse in place of the real king, and was then killed (or exiled) along with the curse. This is an example of substitution sacrifice—transferring evil from an important person to a less important one, to save the former.

[47] Persian Yatukih Sorcery

Yātukīh is a term in ancient Persian for witchcraft, especially harmful magic. In Zoroastrian literature, yātukīh is connected to the forces of evil (Angra Mainyu) and demons. The sorcerer uses demon names, sacrifices, and impure materials to harm others. The struggle between good and evil in Zoroastrianism is cosmic, and witchcraft is a tool in the hands of evil forces.

[48] Zoroastrian Corpse Naming

In Zoroastrian tradition, dead bodies are considered impure. Using the name of a dead person in witchcraft is considered particularly grave, as it mixes the world of impurity with the world of the living. The sorcerer calls the name of the dead and asks them to harm enemies. This is a form of necromancy, but in the Zoroastrian context it takes on the additional meaning of religious impurity.

[49] Roman Curses (Roman Devotio)

Devotio is an ancient Roman ritual where a military commander sacrifices himself and the enemy soldiers to the underworld gods in exchange for victory. The commander would ride into the enemy ranks, surrender his life, and his death would ensure the enemy's defeat. This is an example of self-sacrifice in war magic, where the death of one person brings defeat to many.

[50] Etruscan Curse Tablets

The Etruscans, an ancient culture in Italy, also used lead curse tablets, similar to the Greeks and Romans. Many tablets have been found in the Etruscan language, attesting to a rich magical culture. Etruscan curses include appeals to unique Etruscan gods and incorporate elements from Greek and Roman culture.

[51] Carthaginian Child Sacrifice

Carthage, a Phoenician colony in North Africa, was accused by Greeks and Romans of sacrificing children to the gods. Archaeology at the "tophet" site has revealed large quantities of child and animal bones, alongside inscriptions requesting the favor of the gods. Scholarship is divided on whether this indeed represents systematic child sacrifice. Some believe it is the burial of children who died naturally, while others argue that textual and archaeological evidence supports the existence of sacrifice. The story demonstrates the difficulty of archaeological interpretation of ancient religious practices.

[52] Scapulimancy

Scapulimancy is divination using the shoulder blade (scapula) of animals. The bone is heated or cracked, and the resulting fissures are interpreted as signs of the future. The practice is common in many cultures, including China, North America, and Europe. It reflects the idea that the future is written in nature, and that it can be read through physical signs.

---

Rituals 53-56: Elemental Divination

[53] Geomancy

Geomancy is divination using earth. The diviner throws a handful of earth on the ground or makes marks in sand, and interprets the resulting patterns. In certain traditions, geomancy is also connected to burial and curse rituals—burying an object in a specific place to influence the course of events. This is a connection between divination and active magic.

[54] Hydromancy

Hydromancy is divination using water. The diviner gazes at water (in a spring, lake, or vessel) and interprets movements, sounds, or reflections. In black magic, water is also used for casting curses—placing a curse in a river or sea, or using water to "wash away" the victim's luck. The water carries the curse over distances.

[55] Aeromancy

Aeromancy is divination using air. The diviner observes clouds, wind, or bird flight. In black magic, smoke is used to carry a curse through the air, or spirits are called to act through the air. Air is an invisible element, therefore suitable for hidden magic.

[56] Pyromancy

Pyromancy is divination using fire. The diviner observes flames, smoke, or embers. In black magic, a figure of the victim or objects connected to them are burned, believing that the fire will transfer pain to the victim. Fire symbolizes both destruction and purification.

---

Rituals 57-70: Asian Traditions

[57] Japanese Ushi no Koku Mairi

Ushi no koku mairi is an ancient Japanese ritual, where a person goes to a Shinto or Buddhist shrine during the Hour of the Ox (between 1 and 3 AM), wears white clothes, places a mirror on their head, and drives nails into a sacred tree while cursing their enemy. Each nail represents part of the curse, and the belief is that after seven days, the enemy will die. The ritual reflects the importance of order and precision in Japanese magic, and the belief in the power of sacred places.

[58] Toyol

Toyol is a child spirit in Malay and Indonesian tradition. According to belief, a bomoh (sorcerer) can summon the spirit of a fetus or infant who died, and bind it into a small figurine. The toyol is used for errands, theft, and harming enemies. The owner must feed the toyol with milk or blood, and give it toys. If not properly cared for, the toyol may become dangerous to its owner. This is an example of a serving spirit, common in many Southeast Asian traditions.

[59] Kumanthong

Kumanthong is a child spirit in Thailand, similar to the toyol. The name originates in Thai folklore, where a child with supernatural powers was born. Today, Kumanthong refers to the spirit of a dead child, which has undergone a summoning ritual and been bound into a golden figurine. Believers treat the spirit as a child, feed it, and consult it. Kumanthong can bring luck, wealth, and protection, but also harm enemies. In recent years, the phenomenon has become popular outside Thailand as well, raising ethical discussions.

[60] Luk Thep

Luk Thep is a modern Thai phenomenon, where elaborate baby dolls are believed to contain child spirits. Believers buy expensive dolls, bring them to "spirit placement" rituals at temples, and treat them as real children—feeding them, buying them clothes, and even booking seats for them on airplanes. Believers claim the dolls bring luck, wealth, and protection. The phenomenon has drawn criticism in Thailand, with some viewing it as superstition and economic exploitation.

[61] Susuk

Susuk is a Malay-Javanese practice where gold or silver needles are inserted under the skin, usually in the face, to attain beauty, personal charm, and protection. The needles are "charged" with spells and enchanted by a bomoh (healer). The belief is that the needles give a person attractiveness, confidence, and success. However, the needles can cause harm if not properly cared for, or if the person violates associated taboos. The practice is less common today due to fears of infection and spiritual consequences.

[62] Balian Tenung

A Balian is a healer-shaman in Bali. Some Balian specialize in tenung—black magic. They can cast curses, harm enemies, or send evil spirits. As in other traditions, the Balian can serve both healing and harm, depending on their intention and the client's needs. The distinction between "white Balian" and "black Balian" depends on their reputation and the nature of their actions.

[63] Filipino Kulam

Kulam is a term in the Philippines for witchcraft, especially harmful magic. The mangkukulam is the sorcerer who uses kulam. Rituals include the use of dolls, spells, and materials from the victim. Belief in kulam is still common in rural areas, sometimes leading to accusations and persecution. As in other traditions, kulam serves to explain illness, failures, and disasters.

[64] Mentawai – Soul Stealing

In the Mentawai Islands (Indonesia), the shaman (sikerei) believes that the soul (sima) can be stolen by sorcerers or evil spirits. Soul stealing causes illness, depression, and death. The shaman enters a trance, goes out to seek the stolen soul, and returns it to the body. This is a perception of illness as absence of soul, and healing as soul restoration. The sorcerer who steals souls uses their power for harm, unlike the shaman who serves healing.

[65] Dayak Mandau Curse

The Dayak people of Borneo use the mandau (traditional sword) in curse rituals. The sword is considered to possess magical power, and using it in ritual can direct power against an enemy. The mandau is sometimes decorated with feathers and symbols, enhancing its power. The ritual includes dances, spells, and directing the sword toward the enemy's location.

[66] Igorot – Headhunting Rite

The Igorot people of the northern Philippines historically engaged in ritual headhunting. Heads were taken from enemies and brought to the village, believed to bring power, fertility, and blessing. The ritual included dances, prayers, and burial of heads in special places. Headhunting was also connected to revenge and honor. The practice declined with colonialism and Christianity, but its memory continues to influence Igorot culture.

[67] Ifugao Bultung

The Ifugao people (also in the Philippines) believe in bultung—evil spirits that can harm a person. The sorcerer can send bultung against an enemy, or use amulets to protect against them. Bultung are connected to the world of the dead and nature, and require worship and sacrifices to appease them. This is an animistic perception, where the world is full of spirits that must be considered.

[68] Vedic Vajrakavacha

Vajrakavacha is a Sanskrit term meaning "thunderbolt armor." In Vedic tradition, it refers to an amulet or incantation protecting the warrior from enemy weapons. The use of vajra (thunderbolt) as a symbol of invincible power reflects the importance of war and protection in Vedic culture. There are also spells for harming enemies, connected to the god Indra and war deities.

[69] Aghori Funeral Ash Ritual

The Aghori are an extreme Shaivite sect in India, known for rituals involving cremation ashes, bones, and human flesh. The Aghori believe there is no impure or pure—everything is an expression of Shiva. They dwell in cremation grounds, eat from skull bowls, and smear their bodies with ashes of the dead. Their rituals are intended to achieve spiritual liberation (moksha) and supernatural powers. Despite the shocking image, the Aghori are not necessarily "black magicians"—they are religious ascetics seeking enlightenment through an extreme path.

[70] Kapalika – Skull Worship

The Kapalika are an ancient Shaivite sect, related to the Aghori. Their name comes from "kapala" (skull), and they used skulls as ritual implements. Their rituals included drinking wine from a skull, smearing cremation ashes, and worshiping fierce gods like Bhairava. The Kapalika were perceived as dangerous and heretical by the Brahminical establishment, but they themselves saw their path as a way to liberation. This is an example of how "black magic" can be part of a legitimate religious tradition, from the believers' perspective.

Rituals 71-80: Native American Traditions

[71] Navajo Skinwalker Ritual

In Navajo (Dine) tradition, the skinwalker (yee naaldlooshii) is a witch who changes shape into an animal (wolf, fox, bear) by wearing the animal's skin. The skinwalker uses their power for harm—they can cause illness, death, madness, and disasters. They operate at night and avoid contact with humans. Belief in skinwalkers is still strong among the Navajo, and they avoid speaking about it publicly. This is an example of how black magic is connected to taboo violation and action against the community.

[72] Iroquois False Face Curse

In the Iroquois Confederacy, the False Face Society performs healing rituals using carved wooden masks. The masks represent ancient spirits, and the wearer receives their power. However, there is also danger—if the masks or spirits are not respected, they can cause harm. The "False Face Curse" is a condition where the mask spirit harms a person due to disrespect. This is an expression of the belief that healing power is also dangerous power.

[73] Cherokee Spittle Charm

In Cherokee tradition, saliva is perceived as possessing magical power. The sorcerer collects the victim's saliva (or saliva mixed with earth), and performs harmful rituals upon it. Saliva contains the person's "essence," so harming it harms the person themselves. As in other traditions, this is an expression of the law of contagion. The Cherokee also believed in the power of words, medicinal plants, and amulets.

[74] Aboriginal Kadaitcha Sorcery

The Kadaitcha is a sorcerer-healer in Australian Aboriginal tradition. They possess special powers and can use them for both healing and harm. Kadaitcha shoes are made of feathers and human hair, leaving a unique imprint. The pointing bone (see below) is a central tool. The Kadaitcha operates according to community laws, and harming enemies is done only with justification.

[75] Australian Pointing Bone

The pointing bone is an Aboriginal practice where the sorcerer directs a bone toward the victim while cursing. The bone is "charged" with power through contact with human fat or the sorcerer's blood. The victim, knowing the bone has been pointed at them, may become ill and even die from belief. This is a classic example of the nocebo effect—strong belief in the curse creates real physical symptoms.

[76] Maori Makutu

Makutu is witchcraft in Maori tradition (New Zealand). The sorcerer (tohunga makutu) uses spells, bones of the dead, and personal objects to harm enemies. Makutu can cause illness, death, or disaster. The Maori also believed in "mana"—spiritual power that people and objects carry, which can be directed harmfully. Makutu was a secret and dangerous practice, and the tohunga was careful not to incur the community's wrath.

[77] Samoan Soul Catcher

In Samoa, the sorcerer can "catch" a person's soul (agu) within a special object, thereby harming or controlling them. The soul catcher is an object (sometimes made of coconut fiber or wood) containing magical substances. After soul capture, the victim loses vitality, becomes ill, and dies. The shaman can also release the soul, if they wish. This is a perception of illness as absence of soul, as in Mentawai.

[78] Hawaiian Night Marchers Curse

Night marchers are, in Hawaiian belief, spirits of ancient warriors who march on certain nights. Whoever sees them may die, unless they are a descendant or possess special protection. The Night Marchers curse is a condition where a person is attacked by the spirits due to sin or disrespect. This is an example of magic connected to ancestral spirits and sacred places.

[79] Mapuche Machi Curse

The machi is a shaman-healer in Mapuche tradition (Chile and Argentina). The machi uses medicinal plants, drums, and communication with spirits to heal. However, some machi can also harm—cast curses, send evil spirits, or cause illness. As in shamanism generally, the same power that heals can also harm, depending on intention and context.

[80] Inca Mummy Binding

The Inca (Peru) believed that the mummies of ancestors possessed power. They were specially preserved, fed, and brought out in rituals. In certain cases, mummies were also used for curses—binding or hiding the mummy could harm descendants. The Spanish, who conquered Peru, destroyed many mummies to break the power of local communities. This is an example of how magic and politics intertwine.

Rituals 81-90: Miscellaneous Traditions

[81] Narcotic Divination

Narcotic divination is divination using drugs or psychoactive substances. The shaman or sorcerer consumes a substance (like ayahuasca, peyote, or hallucinogenic mushrooms) and enters a trance, where they see visions and receive information. The information can be used for healing, predicting the future, or harming enemies. Nina Izel warns that "sacred plants like ayahuasca are powerful energy amplifiers. They are neither good nor bad, but amplify the energy of the person working with them." The use of these substances carries serious psychological and physical risks.

[82] Blood Pact Rituals

A blood pact is a covenant between a person and a supernatural entity (or between two people) through the mixing of blood. Blood symbolizes life, and its mixing creates an unbreakable bond. In Western traditions, a blood pact with Satan involves selling one's soul in exchange for earthly power. In other traditions, a blood pact with a spirit or god is meant to ensure their assistance. Psychologically, the blood pact creates deep emotional commitment, difficult to retract.

[83] Blacksmith's Curse

In many African cultures, the blacksmith is perceived as possessing magical powers due to their mastery of fire and metal. A blacksmith's curse is considered especially powerful, as it is backed by the power of fire and the blacksmith's skill. The blacksmith can "curse" weapons or tools, so that anyone using them will be harmed. This is an example of how certain professions are perceived as possessing supernatural power.

[84] Cursing Stones

In Celtic traditions (Ireland, Scotland), special stones are used for casting curses. The stones are placed in sacred sites, and the curser turns them while reciting the curse. The belief is that the stones hold the curse forever. In other areas (like India) as well, there are cursing stones, serving as silent testimony to a tragic event or injustice.

[85] Malignant Herb Magic

Use of poisonous plants or plants with hallucinogenic properties to harm others. The distinction between a medicinal plant and a poisonous plant is often fine, and knowledge of plants is power. The sorcerer can poison an enemy using plants, or use psychoactive plants to alter their consciousness and harm them. The boundary between medicine and poison depends on dosage and intention.

[86] Human Sacrifice

Human sacrifice is the most severe practice in black magic. It has existed in various cultures throughout history—the Aztecs, Maya, Carthage (according to accusations), and others. In most cases, sacrifice was intended to appease gods, ensure good harvests, or strengthen the ruler's power. In the modern era, human sacrifice is extremely rare and is considered a serious crime in every society. Most accusations of human sacrifice are racist stereotypes or blood libels.

[87] Nocturnal Rites

Most black magic rituals are performed at night, sometimes in special places—cemeteries, crossroads, groves. Night symbolizes darkness, the hidden, and the dangerous. It is also a time when humans are less active and spirits more active. Nocturnal rites create a special psychological atmosphere, enhancing the sense of power and mystery.

[88] Sympathetic Magic

Sympathetic magic is an overarching principle unifying many practices. It is based on two laws: the law of similarity (like affects like) and the law of contagion (things that have been in contact continue to affect each other). Practices like voodoo dolls, using the victim's hair, and casting curses on a figure—all are based on sympathetic magic. These principles are universal, appearing in different cultures without connection between them.

[89] Pagan Rituals

"Pagan ritual" is a Christian term referring to non-Christian religions. Christianity viewed worship of pagan gods as devil worship, so many pagan rituals were accused of being black magic. In reality, pagan worship was diverse—some included animal sacrifice, some nature worship, and some fertility rites. The distinction between "religion" and "black magic" is often a matter of perspective.

[90] Cook Islands Gardenia Divination

In the Cook Islands (Polynesia), gardenia flowers are used for divination. The diviner picks a flower and interprets its condition—wilting, color, scent—as a sign of the future. This divination can also be used to identify the source of a curse, or to detect an enemy. This is an example of how nature serves as a magical tool.

Rituals 91-100: Miscellaneous Traditions – Continued

[91] Lead Heart

A lead heart is a magical object used in love curses. The heart is made of lead, and nails or pins are driven into it while calling the victim's name. The purpose is to "bind" the victim's heart to the magician, or to cause them suffering if they do not comply. This is a medieval European practice, combining sympathetic magic with the symbolism of the heart.

[92] Ink Curses

Ink curses are curses written with special ink (sometimes mixed with blood or other substances) on parchment or paper. The parchment is burned, buried, or hidden. The belief is that the written words continue to act even after the parchment is destroyed. This is a form of written curse, combining the power of the written word with the power of materials.

[93] Death Witchcraft

Death witchcraft is intentional work with death energies, spirits of the dead, and symbols of death. This is not necessarily "evil"—"death witchcraft is a branch of occult practice deeply connected to the mysteries of life, death, and the transition between them. It is not concerned with death as destruction, but with death as part of the life cycle." The witch learns to know death, respect it, and use its energies for change and transformation. This is a universal pattern, appearing in Santa Muerte, Voodoo, and other traditions.

[94] Bee Curses

In certain traditions (mainly in Europe), a swarm of bees is sent to harm an enemy. Bees are considered magical creatures, connecting worlds. A bee curse includes spells directed at the bees, guiding them toward the victim. This is an example of using animals as magical messengers.

[95] Fly Binding

Fly binding is a practice appearing in the book "Black Magick Rituals." A live fly is used, tied with thread, and a ritual is performed upon it with the aim of "binding" the enemy and draining them of vitality. The fly symbolizes decay and contamination, and binding it is supposed to transfer these qualities to the enemy. This is an example of using insects in magic.

[96] Cicada Curse

In certain traditions, the cicada symbolizes time and cycles. A cicada curse is intended to "freeze" the enemy in time, prevent them from progressing, or cause them to remain in the same state forever. The cicada is buried or imprisoned, and the curse is recited during the act. This is an example of using symbols of nature in magic.

[97] Dark Moon Scrying

Scrying is divination by gazing into a reflective surface (mirror, water, crystal). Dark moon scrying is performed on a moonless night, or during the black moon phase. Darkness allows vision of the future, spirits, or hidden enemies. This is an example of the connection between time, light, and magic.

[98] Shadow Work

Shadow work is a psychological-spiritual practice, where the person works with their "shadow"—the repressed parts, fears, and dark emotions. In black magic, shadow work can include summoning dark entities, rituals for confronting fears, and using negative energies for growth. This is an example of how "black magic" can also serve personal development, not just harming others.

[99] Dark Ancestor Worship

Ancestor worship is common in many cultures, but "dark ancestor worship" focuses on ancestors who died a violent death, or ancestors with a difficult character. The believer turns to them for help in harming enemies, revenge, or obtaining power. The worship includes sacrifices, use of bones, and name-calling. This is an example of how the same practice (ancestor worship) can serve both good and evil.

[100] Magical Poisons

Magical poisons are substances combining physical poison with magical intention. The sorcerer prepares poison from plants, animals, or minerals, and charges it with spells and intention. The poison is given to the victim in food or drink, or transferred through contact. The boundary between physical poisoning and magical harm is blurred. This is an example of the combination of chemistry and magic.

Part Four: The 20 Universal Patterns – In-Depth Analysis

---

Chapter 5: Introduction to Pattern Analysis

One of the most fascinating questions in the study of black magic is: Why did cultures isolated from each other develop strikingly similar practices? How is it that Aboriginal Australians, medieval European villagers, and African tribes all used the law of similarity, the law of contagion, and the belief in the ability to harm from a distance?

Dousset suggests that "magic is a place where belonging and being are redefined, and therefore a place where concepts of 'person,' 'group,' 'ethnicity,' or 'power' are redefined and adapted to changing historical and material conditions." Black magic is not just a technique for harm, but a social-cognitive mechanism for dealing with change, conflict, and uncertainty.

In this chapter, we will analyze 20 universal patterns recurring in different cultures. Each pattern will be presented through:

· Definition and basic principles
· Examples from different cultures
· Psychological analysis of the mechanisms involved
· Anthropological analysis of the social role

---

Chapter 6: The 20 Universal Patterns

---

Pattern 1: The Law of Similarity

Definition

The law of similarity, or homeopathic magic, is the belief that things resembling each other affect each other. If I want to harm a person, I will harm a figure representing them. If I want to heal, I will use a plant resembling the diseased organ. This principle was first systematically described by anthropologist Sir James George Frazer in his book "The Golden Bough" (1890).

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Poppet figures made of wax, cloth, or wood, into which pins are stuck or which are tied. Archaeological examples have been found from the Roman period through the 19th century.

African traditions: Nkisi figures in Congo—wooden statues with nails, each nail representing a request or curse. The bokor in Haitian Voodoo uses cloth dolls to represent victims.

Malay traditions: A clay figure the size of a foot, into which palm branches are stuck while calling the victim's name. The Malay ritual includes burying the figure in the path the victim is expected to pass.

Native American traditions: Among Algonquian tribes, shamans create small wooden figures of enemies and destroy them in fire.

Australian traditions: The pointing bone is not a figure, but it operates on a similar principle—the bone itself represents the sorcerer's power and is directed toward the victim.

Psychological Analysis

The law of similarity is based on several universal cognitive mechanisms:

1. Associative thinking: The human brain naturally connects similar things. This is a basic cognitive tendency, effective in most contexts, but can lead to errors in causality.
2. Nocebo effect: The victim who believes their figure is being harmed may develop real symptoms. Samuel Nelson explains that "the influence radiates thoughts that appear as rational conclusions or sudden 'insights' about the injustice of their situation."
3. Confirmation bias: After a person believes they are a curse victim, they will notice every negative event as proof, ignoring natural explanations.
4. Anxiety and control: Performing a ritual on a figure gives a sense of control and agency in the face of a chaotic situation. Both the performer and the victim experience a sense of order in the world.

Anthropological Analysis

Anthropologically, the law of similarity plays important social roles:

1. Conflict regulation: In societies without formal legal systems, the threat of a curse can deter harm and provide a mechanism for dispute resolution. Belief in the power of the curse creates social order.
2. Expression of intense emotions: Harm rituals provide an outlet for emotions like envy, anger, and frustration, in a way that may prevent immediate physical violence.
3. Explanation of inequality and bad luck: Why does one person succeed and another fail? Why does illness strike one and not another? Belief in black magic provides an explanation for injustice and suffering, enabling psychological coping.
4. Group boundaries: Accusations of black magic often define boundaries between "us" and "them." The witch is always the other—the stranger, the different one, the deviant from the norm.

---

Pattern 2: The Law of Contagion

Definition

The law of contagion, or contagious magic, is the belief that things that have been in contact with each other continue to affect each other even after physical contact is severed. Hair, nails, blood, saliva, clothing, or photographs—all are considered to contain the person's "essence" and can be used to harm or heal them.

Examples from Different Cultures

Cherokee traditions: The sorcerer follows their victim until they spit on the ground. They collect the saliva mixed with earth on a stick, insert it into a tube made from a poisonous plant along with seven earthworms and wood splinters from a lightning-struck tree, and bury it all under fire.

European traditions: Witch bottles often contained the victim's urine, along with nails, pins, and pieces of glass. The bottle was buried or placed in the hearth, aiming to cause pain to the witch who sent the curse.

African traditions: Use of nails, hair, and blood in curses and protection rituals. In Haitian Voodoo, a "packet" containing materials from the victim is used to control them.

Greco-Roman traditions: Curse tablets (defixiones) sometimes included hair or pieces of the victim's clothing, along with the text.

Australian traditions: The pointing bone is "charged" through contact with human fat or the sorcerer's own blood, and from there the "power" (arungquiltha) is transferred to the victim through the air.

Psychological Analysis

The law of contagion reflects several psychological phenomena:

1. Emotional contagion: People recoil from touching objects that belonged to a person considered "impure" or "dangerous," even without any rational basis. The feeling of "impurity" is universal.
2. Magical thinking: The tendency to believe that thoughts can affect the world, or that a part represents the whole. This is a normal cognitive tendency, especially strong in children, but continues to affect adults in stressful situations.
3. Disgust: The evolutionary disgust system is designed to protect us from disease and contamination. It is triggered by bodily substances (blood, feces, saliva), and may spread to objects that have come into contact with them. Black magic exploits this mechanism.
4. Nocebo effect: The victim who knows that materials from their body have been used against them may develop real symptoms, due to powerful belief.

Anthropological Analysis

1. The body as battlefield: The human body is perceived as a microcosm of society. Harm to body parts symbolizes harm to the person and their social status.
2. Technologies of the self: Use of bodily substances in rituals reflects perceptions of identity and the "essence" of the person. What is a person? Are they only the physical body, or is there an invisible "essence"?
3. Risk management: Collection of a person's bodily substances constitutes a risk to them, so people are careful not to leave hair, nails, or blood in public places. This is effective risk management, even if based on mistaken belief.
4. Memory and presence: Bodily substances serve as a kind of material memory of the person, enabling their "presence" even when they are not there.

Pattern 3: Sacrifice

Definition

Sacrifice—of a person, animal, or valuable object—is one of the oldest and most widespread religious practices. In black magic, sacrifice is intended to appease entities, strengthen a curse, or obtain power. The sacrifice is perceived as currency with the supernatural world: I give you something precious, and you give me what I desire.

Examples from Different Cultures

Carthaginian and Phoenician traditions: According to Greek and Roman sources (biased and hostile sources), the Phoenicians sacrificed children to the god Moloch. Archaeology at the "tophet" site in Carthage has revealed large quantities of child and animal bones, alongside inscriptions requesting the favor of the gods. Modern scholarship is divided on whether this indeed represents systematic child sacrifice.

Aztec traditions: Sacrifice of human hearts to the sun god, based on the belief that blood nourishes the gods and maintains the sun's movement. Victims were sometimes prisoners of war, sometimes volunteers from the community. The ritual involved removing the heart in a surgical ceremony.

Palo Mayombe traditions: Use of human bones (usually from the deceased) in building an "nkisi"—a sacred object containing power. The tradition, originating in Congo, sometimes includes animal sacrifice. It is important to emphasize: Palo Mayombe does not include human sacrifice, contrary to racist stereotypes.

Ancient Indian traditions: Horse sacrifice (ashvamedha) in Vedic tradition—a complex ritual where the horse sacrifice symbolized the king's power and the land's fertility.

Voodoo traditions: Sacrifice of chickens, goats, or other animals, as a way to nourish the loa (spirits) and receive their help. The blood is offered to the gods, and the meat is eaten by the participants.

Psychological Analysis

1. Investment principle: The more precious the sacrifice, the more we believe the outcome will be significant. This is a form of economic thinking applied to the supernatural world.
2. Cognitive dissonance: After sacrificing something precious, we must believe the sacrifice was meaningful, otherwise we experience loss and foolishness. Therefore, belief strengthens precisely after the sacrifice.
3. Guilt and atonement: Sacrifice may serve as atonement for guilt, a way to appease conscience or atone for sins.
4. Symbolism and empathy: Animal sacrifice evokes empathetic feelings, but also allows emotional distance. The ability to sacrifice demonstrates power and control over emotions.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Economy of the sacred: Sacrifice creates an exchange system with the supernatural world. As in regular economy, there is demand and supply, currency (the sacrifice), and "interest" (future benefit).
2. Group solidarity: Shared sacrifice rituals create a sense of community and unity. The communal eating of the sacrifice strengthens social bonds.
3. Hierarchy and power: The ability to make precious sacrifices (especially human) demonstrates power and wealth. Rulers made impressive sacrifices to display their power.
4. Sacred violence: Sacrifice frames violence as a sacred and necessary act. It permits killing in a religious context, creating a distinction between "murder" (forbidden) and "sacrifice" (permitted).
5. Stereotypes and racism: It is important to emphasize that accusations of human sacrifice have been used throughout history to defame ethnic and religious groups. The blood libel against Jews, accusations against witches in Europe, and stereotypes about African Voodoo—all reflect the same phenomenon: making the other monstrous to justify persecution.

Pattern 4: Necromancy and Spirit Communication

Definition

Communication with the dead is a universal belief that the ability to speak with those who are no more can grant knowledge, power, or the ability to harm. The dead are perceived as closer to the supernatural world, and therefore can mediate between worlds or act on behalf of the living.

Examples from Different Cultures

Ancient Greek traditions: Nekyomanteia—summoning the dead for divination. The most famous site was at Ephyra, where Odysseus is believed to have summoned the spirit of Tiresias. The ritual included digging a pit, offering blood, and prayers.

Medieval European traditions: Use of skulls, bones, and graves in witchcraft rituals. The belief was that spirits of the dead, especially those who died a violent death or were not properly buried, could be used for magical purposes.

Chinese traditions: Ancestor worship includes feeding the dead and consulting them. In certain traditions, it is believed that hungry spirits can harm the living if not properly cared for.

Thai traditions: Kumanthong—spirit of a dead child, usually a fetus removed from the womb and buried in a special ritual. The spirit is adopted as a "child" and used for protection, wealth, or harming enemies. A similar ritual exists in Malaysia (toyol).

Maori traditions: Makutu—witchcraft including the use of bones of the dead and the power of mana. The sorcerer can direct the spirit of the dead to harm an enemy.

Voodoo traditions: Work with Baron Samedi, loa of death, to gain power over life and death. Death worship includes the use of bones, skulls, and graveyard earth.

Psychological Analysis

1. Coping with loss: Communication with the dead provides comfort to mourners and helps in coping with grief. The ability to "speak" with the dead maintains connection and eases the sense of separation.
2. Seeking beyond knowledge: The dead "know" what we do not know—what happens after death. They are perceived as having access to hidden knowledge.
3. Projection and illusion: Voices "from the dead" are often projections of our wishes and fears. The person hears what they want to hear.
4. Dissonance resolution: Unexplained death (murder, accident) creates dissonance. If the dead can "tell" what happened, the world becomes comprehensible again.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Identity continuity: Ancestor worship enables continuity of group identity. The ancestors continue to be part of the community, even after death.
2. Symbolic rule: Rulers sometimes use ancestor worship to establish their authority. If the king is a descendant of powerful ancestors, their authority is natural.
3. Memory and history: The dead are a "living archive" of collective memory. Through them, the community remembers its history.
4. Liminal space: The dead are on the threshold—between the world of the living and the other world. As such, they are ideal mediators between worlds, assisting in transitions and changes.
5. Ethics of respect: In most traditions, work with the dead requires respect and adherence to rules. Whoever breaks the rules may be harmed by angry spirits. This is a social mechanism for maintaining the sanctity of the dead.

Pattern 5: Pointing and Distant Cursing

Definition

Use of a bone, stick, or other object directed toward the victim to cast a curse, without need for physical contact or representational figure. The power resides in the object itself, the sorcerer's intention, and the words of the curse.

Examples from Different Cultures

Australian traditions (Arunta): The sorcerer withdraws to an isolated area, places the pointing bone (irna) on the ground, and whispers a curse over it: "May your heart be torn." Then he approaches where his victim is, spies on them in the dark, and shakes the bone toward them while cursing. The power, arungquiltha, passes to the victim and causes illness and death.

Melanesian traditions (Vanuatu): John, a resident of South Malekula, believed that two elders tried to harm him through a curse. Suspicion arose when they invited him to drink kava and were meticulous about suspicious details. Later he found an unfamiliar plant in his garden—a sign, he said, that something had been buried there to harm him.

Native American traditions: Certain tribes used a "spirit stick"—a carved stick with feathers and markings, pointed toward the enemy while calling their name.

European traditions: "Curse stick" or "witch's staff"—a wooden rod used for concentrating and directing magical energy.

African traditions: A charmed bone or animal horn containing magical substances, pointed toward the victim.

Psychological Analysis

1. Strongest nocebo effect: Knowing that a bone has been pointed at you in a curse can create extreme stress reactions, especially in societies where belief in the power of the curse is absolute.
2. Power of intention: The belief that intention alone can harm—the "evil eye"—is universal. The look, the gesture, the word—all are perceived as possessing power.
3. Internalization and self-blame: The victim may internalize the curse and blame themselves: "I deserve it," "I am guilty." This is a way to cope with feelings of helplessness.
4. Collective nocebo effect: When the entire community believes in the power of the curse, social pressure and collective fear increase symptoms in the victim.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Power and distance: The ability to harm from a distance is an expression of supernatural power. The greater the distance, the more impressive the power. This is a kind of "technology" of control.
2. Conflict regulation: The threat of a curse deters harm and provides a mechanism for dispute resolution without physical violence. The pointed bone is like a drawn weapon.
3. Responsibility and denial: The sorcerer can deny involvement—"it's the bone, not me." The object bears responsibility.
4. Cosmology of power: The pointing bone represents a perception of power as tangible, as something that can be stored in an object and directed. This is a material perception of the supernatural.
5. Change and social tension: As the Vanuatu case shows, witchcraft accusations often arise in times of change and tension—return from the city, change in status, land conflicts.

Pattern 6: Written Curses

Definition

Use of writing as a means of casting a curse. The written word is perceived as having special power—it is permanent, binding, and carries the writer's intention even after their death.

Examples from Different Cultures

Greco-Roman traditions: Lead curse tablets (defixiones), on which the victim's name, details of the curse, and sometimes appeals to gods or demons were written. The tablet was folded, pierced with a nail, and deposited in a grave, well, or temple. Thousands have been found, curses against legal opponents, athletic competitors, fleeing lovers, and thieves.

Ancient Egyptian traditions: Curse inscriptions on pottery ("execration texts")—names of Egypt's enemies were written on vessels, then smashed, believing this would destroy the enemy.

Babylonian traditions: Seals and reliefs with curses against whoever damages them. The curse is directed toward the future—whoever destroys the seal will be harmed.

Medieval European traditions: Curses written on parchment, sometimes combined with magical symbols, buried or placed in churches.

Jewish traditions: Amulets with sacred names and angel names, sometimes for protection and sometimes for harm. Practical Kabbalah books contain formulas for harming enemies.

Psychological Analysis

1. Power of writing: The written word is perceived as having greater validity than the spoken word. It is permanent, official, binding.
2. Permanence and eternity: The written curse continues to act even after the writer's death. It is eternal.
3. Detail and precision: Writing allows precise detailing of identity and desired harm. There is no room for error.
4. Writing ritual: The act of writing itself is a magical act. The writer concentrates their intention, engraves the words, thereby "fixing" the curse.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Literacy and power: Societies transitioning from orality to literacy sometimes attribute special power to writing. Writing is perceived as magical, because it enables communication with absentees and the future.
2. Anonymity and security: A written curse allows anonymity. The victim does not know who cursed them, protecting the magician.
3. Archaeology of emotion: Curse tablets are archaeological evidence of human emotions—envy, anger, frustration, unrequited love. They are a window into the human soul in antiquity.
4. Alternative forums: In societies without effective legal systems, written curses served as a way to obtain justice. The victim appeals to the gods, because they cannot appeal to humans.

Pattern 7: Shapeshifting and Lycanthropy

Definition

The belief that witches can change their form into animals—wolves, owls, cats, and others. The animal form allows them to move freely, spy, and attack without being discovered.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Belief in werewolves—humans who turn into wolves at night and prey. Many witch trials included accusations of lycanthropy. The belief was that the skin remained behind, or that the change occurred with the help of a magical ointment.

Navajo traditions: Skinwalker—a witch who wears the skin of an animal (wolf, fox, bear) and changes shape. The skinwalker can run fast, climb walls, and sneak. They are used to harm enemies, steal, and perform forbidden acts.

African traditions: Belief in witches who turn into hyenas, leopards, or crocodiles. In Zaïre, belief in leopard men—cults that could turn into leopards and prey.

Brazilian traditions: Lobisomem—a Brazilian version of the werewolf, connected to seventh children in a family.

Indian traditions: Naga—magical snakes that can turn into humans, and humans who can turn into snakes.

Psychological Analysis

1. Repression and projection: Forbidden impulses (violence, sexuality) are projected onto the monstrous "other." The werewolf represents the animality within us.
2. Dual identity: The fantasy of double life—human by day, animal by night—reflects the complexity of human identity.
3. Fear of boundary blurring: The boundary between human and animal is a basic boundary. Its blurring creates existential anxiety.
4. Dehumanization: Accusation of lycanthropy is a way to make a person non-human, thereby justifying persecution.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Boundaries of humanity: Every society defines what is "human." The shape-shifting witch violates this definition, and is therefore dangerous.
2. Power and control: The ability to change shape is supernatural power, but also loss of control over the self. The tension between power and loss of control is central.
3. Category blurring: Lévi-Strauss showed that mythological thinking deals with blurring boundaries between categories. Werewolf blurs the boundary between nature and culture, human and animal.
4. Ecology and fear: In areas with dangerous predators (wolves in Europe, leopards in Africa), belief in lycanthropy reflects real fear of predators, and transfers it to the figure of the witch.

Pattern 8: Poisons and Plants

Definition

Use of poisonous plants, drugs, and other natural substances for harm, control, or consciousness alteration. The boundary between medicine and poison, between healing and harm, is often fine.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Witch ointments (flying ointments)—mixtures of psychoactive plants like belladonna, scopolamine, and mandrake, in animal fat. Witches "flew" after applying the ointment—in reality, they experienced hallucinatory trips.

African traditions: Use of poisonous plants to poison enemies. The distinction between "poison" and "medicine" depends on context and dosage.

South American traditions: Ayahuasca—a hallucinogenic brew used by shamans for healing and communicating with the spirit world. Nina Izel explains that "one of the biggest mistakes about sacred plants is that they are benevolent entities that will heal you and cannot harm you. It's important to understand that sacred plants like ayahuasca are powerful energy amplifiers. They are neither good nor bad, but amplify the energy of the person working with them."

Chinese traditions: Gu—a complex poison made by placing venomous animals (snake, scorpion, centipede) in a sealed container until they devour each other, then using the resulting liquid to harm enemies. This is both a magical practice and a real poison.

Ancient Greek traditions: Use of hemlock for execution, but also belief that certain plants could be used to communicate with the dead.

Psychological Analysis

1. Consciousness alteration and mystical experience: Psychoactive substances produce intense experiences interpreted as encounters with higher worlds.
2. Power and knowledge: Knowledge of plants is power. Whoever knows to distinguish between poison and medicine, between hallucinogenic and lethal plants, is highly valuable.
3. Symbolism of boundaries: Certain plants grow at boundaries—between field and forest, between culture and nature—and are therefore perceived as possessing magical power.
4. Hallucination and reality: It is difficult to distinguish between hallucination and reality. Experiences under the influence of drugs are very convincing, reinforcing cosmological beliefs.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Ecology of knowledge: Every society has accumulated rich knowledge about plants in its environment. This knowledge passes from generation to generation, used for both healing and harm.
2. Shamanism and healing: The shaman is an expert in consciousness alteration. They use plants to enter different states of consciousness, communicate with spirits, and diagnose illnesses.
3. Ethics of use: In most societies, using plants for harm is perceived as forbidden. The sorcerer who uses knowledge for evil is a "bokor"—a dangerous expert.
4. Modernity and tradition: In the modern era, traditional use of psychoactive plants conflicts with anti-drug laws and Western perceptions of mental health.

Pattern 9: Child Spirits and Fetal Worship

Definition

In several cultures in Southeast Asia and Africa, there is worship of spirits of children who died—especially fetuses or infants. The spirits are adopted as "children" and used for protection, wealth, or harm.

Examples from Different Cultures

Thai traditions: Kumanthong—spirit of a dead child, usually a fetus, buried in a special ritual. The body is dried or burned, and the spirit is "imprisoned" in a figurine or doll. The owner feeds the spirit, gives it toys, and consults it. Kumanthong can bring luck, wealth, and also harm enemies.

Malay traditions: Toyol—a similar spirit, of a dead child, used for theft and harm. The toyol is sent to steal money or objects, or to frighten enemies. The owner must feed it with milk or blood.

Modern Thai traditions: Luk Thep—baby dolls believed to contain spirits. In recent years, the Luk Thep phenomenon has become popular in Thailand, where people buy expensive dolls, treat them as real children (feed them, buy them clothes), and believe they bring luck.

African traditions: In certain areas, it is believed that spirits of dead children can be used by the sorcerer.

Psychological Analysis

1. Coping with child loss: The worship allows people to cope with miscarriage or infant death. The spirit continues to "live" and is cared for.
2. Projection of needs: The believer's emotional needs are projected onto the spirit. The spirit needs food, love, attention—exactly as a real child would.
3. Fantasy of power: Control over a spirit gives a sense of power, especially to people lacking power in society.
4. Anthropomorphism: The human tendency to attribute human qualities to objects and spirits is especially strong when dealing with child forms.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Cosmology of birth and death: The worship reflects perceptions of birth, death, and continuity. The dead child does not disappear, but continues to exist in the spirit world.
2. Economy and livelihood: The toyol is used for theft—a way for the poor to obtain livelihood. The spirit replaces the work the child could have done.
3. Modernity and tradition: The modern Luk Thep phenomenon demonstrates how traditional beliefs adapt to the modern world. The dolls look like regular dolls, but are believed to contain spirits.
4. Ethics of spirit adoption: The worship raises ethical questions: Is it right to "bind" the spirit of a dead child? Does the child want to be enslaved to an adult? The traditions themselves address these questions.

Pattern 10: Blood Sacrifice and Blood Pacts

Definition

Blood is the fluid of life. In many traditions, blood is perceived as carrying life force, the soul, identity. Use of blood in curses and rituals is a way to activate powerful force.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Blood pact with the devil—signing in blood a contract, where the sorcerer sells their soul in exchange for power. The Faust legend is the most famous example.

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Palo Mayombe uses animal blood (and sometimes human blood, according to accusations) in creating nkisi—power objects.

Ancient traditions: Use of sacrificial blood to nourish the gods. Blood is poured on the altar, and the gods "drink" it.

Modern traditions: Some modern satanic cults include the use of blood in rituals.

Psychological Analysis

1. Disgust and power: Blood evokes disgust, but precisely because of this it is perceived as powerful. Taboo violation is a way to obtain power.
2. Irrevocable commitment: Signing in blood is absolute commitment. There is no going back.
3. Biological identity: Blood carries biological identity—DNA. Its use in ritual reflects an intuitive perception of biological identity.
4. Life and death: Blood symbolizes life, but shedding blood symbolizes death. This tension creates symbolic power.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Sacrifice and exchange: Blood is a substitute for life. In blood sacrifice, the person gives up something precious (an animal, or in extreme cases, a human) in exchange for divine favor.
2. Kinship and affinity: Blood pact creates artificial kinship. The belief is that mixed blood creates a family bond.
3. Taboo and power: What is forbidden is also powerful. Society forbids shedding human blood, so precisely the forbidden act has the greatest power.
4. Hierarchy and power: The ability to make blood sacrifices demonstrates wealth and power. Rulers made many sacrifices to display their power.

Pattern 11: Graveyard Curses

Definition

Use of graveyard earth, bones of the dead, or objects from graves in curse rituals. The dead are perceived as close to the supernatural world, so graveyard earth carries special power.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Collecting earth from the grave of someone who died a violent death, and using it in curse rituals.

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Palo Mayombe uses human bones (from the deceased) in building nkisi. The dead are "employed" by the magician and serve them.

Australian traditions: Bones of the dead are used in the pointing bone.

Chinese traditions: Feng shui of graves—the location of the grave affects the living. Graves of ancestors are a source of power.

Psychological Analysis

1. Fear of the dead: Fear of the dead is universal. Using them in curses increases the power of fear.
2. Impurity and sanctity: The cemetery is both impure and sacred. This ambiguity amplifies power.
3. Taboo violation: Entering a cemetery at night, digging a grave, taking a bone—all are serious taboo violations. The violation itself is a source of power.
4. Continuity: The dead somehow continue to "live." They can hear, see, and act.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Sanctity of the dead: In most societies, the dead are sacred. Harming a grave is a serious offense against the honor of the dead and their family.
2. Boundaries between worlds: The cemetery is a liminal place—between the world of the living and the world of the dead. In such liminal places, supernatural power is especially strong.
3. Collective memory: Graves are a place of collective memory. Using them in curses is an attempt to use memory to harm in the present.
4. Warning to the magician: In the book "Black Magick Rituals," a warning appears: "In every place (cemetery, crossroads, railway track or forest) there is a guardian entity. Always ask permission to leave a sacrifice there. Light a candle to that entity." Even in curses, there is respect for the dead and the guardians.

Pattern 12: Crossroads

Definition

A crossroads is a liminal place, where different roads meet, and therefore it is perceived as a place where supernatural power is especially strong. Here demons, gods, and spirits are encountered.

Examples from Different Cultures

Greek traditions: Hecate, goddess of witchcraft, is connected to crossroads. There, sacrifices are made to her.

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Eshú in Palo Mayombe and Candomblé is the guardian of crossroads. Before every ritual, a sacrifice is offered to him and permission is asked. "When you go to a crossroads to leave a command, ask Eshú for permission, and leave a candle offering to Eshú, guardian of crossroads."

European traditions: A crossroads is a place where the devil is met, or a place where criminals and witches are buried.

West African traditions: Legba is the god who opens the gate between the human world and the world of the gods. He stands at the crossroads.

Psychological Analysis

1. Choice and decision: A crossroads symbolizes a point of choice. The fear and tension of decision are projected onto the place itself.
2. Boundary and uncertainty: Where roads meet, there is no defined direction. Uncertainty creates anxiety, and anxiety seeks supernatural explanation.
3. Potential: At the crossroads, all roads are open. This is a place of potential, therefore suitable for rituals aimed at change.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Liminal space: A crossroads is a liminal place, neither-here-nor-there. In anthropological theory, such liminal places are places of power and danger.
2. Communication between worlds: The crossroads is a place where different worlds meet—like roads meet. Therefore it is suitable for communication with higher worlds.
3. Social order: The crossroads is outside the village, outside the protected space. It represents the danger outside society, and the power that can be obtained there.
4. Ritual and ceremony: In many traditions, rituals at crossroads require permission from the guardian. This is a mechanism that protects the magician and regulates the ritual.

Pattern 13: Animal Curses

Definition

Use of animals—alive or dead—in curse rituals. Animals serve as sacrifices, messengers, or symbols.

Examples from Different Cultures

Norse traditions: Horse head on a pole (nithing pole)—a curse involving placing a horse's head on a pole in front of the enemy's house.

West African traditions: Sacrifice of chickens, goats, and other animals in Voodoo and Santería.

European traditions: Use of frogs, black cats, and roosters in witchcraft rituals.

Modern traditions: A curse involving a fly, spider, and spider webs—"to bind an enemy and drain their vitality."

Brazilian traditions: Use of small animals (like birds) as sacrifices in Quimbanda.

Psychological Analysis

1. Substitution sacrifice: Animal sacrifice is a substitute for human sacrifice. The animal suffers in place of the human.
2. Animal symbolism: Each animal has symbolic meaning—snake (cunning), owl (wisdom/death), fly (decay). The choice of a specific animal enhances the curse's meaning.
3. Projection: Animal qualities are projected onto the victim—"run around like a headless chicken," "be slow as a snail."
4. Disgust: Use of insects and "dirty" animals increases the sense of disgust and harm.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Human-animal relations: Sacrifice positions animals in a relationship with humans and gods. The animal is a mediator.
2. Economy of sacrifices: Animals have economic value. Their sacrifice is a sacrifice of property, indicating the intensity of desire.
3. Ecology of magic: Animals used in curses are usually local animals, familiar to the community. Magic uses the immediate environment.
4. Modern ethics: Use of animals in curses raises ethical questions in the modern world. In the book "Black Magick Rituals," the author emphasizes: "I do not mention sacrifice of four-legged animals, and the reader is not obligated to perform these rituals. There are dozens of others without blood use in the book."

Pattern 14: Sexuality and Sexual Rituals

Definition

Use of sexuality, sexual fluids, and sexual ritual in black magic ceremonies. Sexuality is perceived as a powerful force, and as taboo-breaking worship.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Osculum Infame—the "shameful kiss"—kissing the devil's anus, as an expression of absolute submission. This is an Inquisition accusation, not a documented practice.

Indian traditions: Tantra—use of sexuality to achieve enlightenment. The boundary between "white" and "black" in Tantra is fine, and some traditions use sex in rituals.

Modern traditions: Some satanic cults include ritual sex.

Popular traditions: Use of semen, menstrual blood, and other bodily fluids in love and binding spells.

Psychological Analysis

1. Power and boundary-breaking: Sexuality is a powerful force, and sexual taboo violation enhances the sense of power.
2. Intimacy and closeness: Use of sexual fluids creates a sense of closeness and absolute control over the victim.
3. **Shame and humiliation": "Dirty" sexual ritual humiliates participants and enhances the sense of submission.
4. Fantasy and reality: Accusations of ritual sex often reflect repressed sexual fantasies, not reality.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Control over body: Sexuality is an expression of control over the body—of the magician and the victim. Use of bodily fluids is an extension of the law of contagion.
2. Taboo and power: Sexuality is one of the strongest taboos in many societies. Its violation grants power, but also risk.
3. Accusations as social tool: The Inquisition used accusations of ritual sex to justify witch persecution. This is a recurring pattern—accusations of deviant sexuality are directed against marginalized groups.
4. Gender and power: In most accusations, the witch is a woman, and female sexuality is perceived as dangerous. This is an expression of patriarchy and fear of female power.

Pattern 15: Love Curses and Binding

Definition

Use of magic to win love, bind one person to another, prevent betrayal, or separate couples. This is one of the most common categories in magical rituals.

Examples from Different Cultures

Egyptian traditions: Love potions and use of amulets to attract a beloved.

Greco-Roman traditions: Curse tablets intended to "bind" a woman to a man, or to separate a couple.

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Many binding rituals in Santería, Voodoo, and Quimbanda. "Powerful love binding, wrapping, separating two people, turning their head."

European traditions: Love potions from plants, use of hair and nails in love spells.

Modern traditions: Binding rituals including candles, photographs, and personal objects.

Psychological Analysis

1. Helplessness in love: Unrequited love is a difficult experience. Turning to magic gives a sense of control in an uncontrollable situation.
2. Jealousy and obsession: Love curses often reflect jealousy and obsession, not true love.
3. Projection: The believer projects their feelings onto the victim—"she must love me, someone just bound her."
4. Refusal to accept rejection: A love curse is a way to refuse rejection, to continue controlling even when the relationship is over.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Marriage and social bonds: In many traditions, marriage is an economic-social matter, not just personal. A love curse can change entire social relationships.
2. Gender and power: Women are often the main users of love spells, because they have less social power to choose a partner.
3. Ethics of binding: Is it permissible to "bind" a person against their will? Most traditions see this as problematic, but still perform it.
4. Magical economy: Love spells are a sought-after "product" in the magic market. Sorcerers and healers make a living from selling them.

Pattern 16: Protection and Breaking Curses

Definition

Just as there is magic for harm, there is magic for protection. Amulets, witch bottles, cleansing rituals, and returning a curse to the sender—all are part of the magical protection system.

Examples from Different Cultures

European traditions: Witch bottles for protection—the bottle attracts the curse and harms the witch who sent it.

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Discharge baths, amulets, and protection rituals. "Before doing black magic work, always light a white candle to your personal orisha and ask that everything go well."

Middle Eastern traditions: Amulets with sacred names, mezuzahs, and hamsas for protection against the evil eye.

Modern traditions: "Curse reversal" rituals—returning negative energy to the sender.

Psychological Analysis

1. Sense of security: Belief in amulets and protection reduces anxiety and provides a psychological sense of security.
2. Placebo effect: The amulet works because people believe it works. The belief itself calms and strengthens.
3. Taking responsibility: Protection rituals allow a person to take responsibility for their protection, instead of feeling a helpless victim.
4. Disguised revenge: Returning a curse to the sender allows revenge under the guise of self-defense.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Protection systems: Every society develops protection systems against evil forces. These reflect the society's perceptions of danger.
2. Protection market: Just as there is demand for harm, there is demand for protection. Sorcerers and healers also sell protection services.
3. **Magical hygiene": Cleansing baths and purification rituals are a kind of "magical hygiene"—maintaining energetic cleanliness.
4. Ethics of protection: Is it permissible to return a curse? This is an ethical dilemma in many traditions. Some argue that returning a curse is preferable to physical revenge.

Pattern 17: Elemental Curses

Definition

Use of the four elements—fire, water, earth, air—in curse rituals. Each element carries symbolic meaning and magical power.

Examples from Different Cultures

Pyromancy: Divination using fire. Burning a figure of the victim in fire to cause them pain.

Hydromancy: Divination using water. Burying a curse in a river or sea, or using water to "wash away" the victim's luck.

Geomancy: Divination using earth. Burying a curse in the ground, or using graveyard earth.

Aeromancy: Divination using air. Curses in the wind, or using smoke to carry a curse.

Psychological Analysis

1. Power of nature: The elements are powerful natural forces, greater than humans. Using them enhances the curse.
2. Universal symbolism: Fire (destruction, purification), water (flow, washing), earth (burial, stability), air (movement, wind)—all are universal symbols.
3. Connection to nature: Use of elements connects the magician to nature and its forces.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Cosmology of elements: Many cultures develop perceptions of a world built from elements. Their use in curses reflects a cosmological perception.
2. Sacred places: Springs, mountains, caves, and other natural places are perceived as possessing power. There, rituals are performed.
3. Ecology and magic: Magic uses the natural environment, and reflects society's relationship with nature.

---

Pattern 18: Death Worship

Definition

Worship of the dead, work with death energies, and use of death as magical power. This is not necessarily "evil," but recognition of the power of death.

Examples from Different Cultures

Mexican traditions: Santa Muerte—"Holy Death," a folk saint in Mexico. Drug dealers, criminals, and people on the margins of society turn to her for protection.

Voodoo traditions: Baron Samedi—loa of death, depicted as a man with a top hat and sunglasses. He guards the dead and can grant life or death.

Indian traditions: Shamshan shamans—sorcerers working in cremation grounds, using cremation ashes for harm or healing.

Modern traditions: "Death witchcraft"—intentional work with death energies, not necessarily for harm. "Death witchcraft is a branch of occult practice deeply connected to the mysteries of life, death, and the transition between them."

Psychological Analysis

1. Coping with death: Death worship is a way to cope with fear of death, to acknowledge it as part of life.
2. Power and control: Working with death gives a sense of control over the uncontrollable.
3. Ambivalence: Death is both frightening and attractive. Worship expresses this ambivalence.
4. Empowerment of margins: People on society's margins sometimes turn to death worship because it accepts them as they are.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Sanctity of death: In all societies, death is a sacred event. Death worship is an extension of this sanctity.
2. The final boundary: Death is the impassable boundary. Working with it is an attempt to cross boundaries.
3. Marginal communities: Death worship often attracts marginal communities—the poor, criminals, prostitutes—who have no other protection. Santa Muerte is an excellent example.
4. Ethics of death: Death witchcraft requires respect and care. "Although death witchcraft may seem mysterious or dark, it is not evil. In fact, it is an inherently respectful practice, seeking to understand, honor, and accept the cycles of the natural world."

Pattern 19: Temporal and Astral Curses

Definition

Choosing an appropriate time for casting a curse—moon phase, star positions, certain hours of the day or night. The belief that time has magical quality.

Examples from Different Cultures

Babylonian traditions: Magical astrology—choosing an appropriate time according to planetary positions.

European traditions: Halloween, Walpurgis Night, and Midsummer—special times for witchcraft.

Modern traditions: Working with dark moon, waning moon. "Perform this ritual at night during dark moon/waning moon."

Afro-Caribbean traditions: Choosing an appropriate time according to the victim's astrological chart. "Tips you won't find in other books: how to analyze your enemy's astrological chart and choose the best time to attack with witchcraft."

Psychological Analysis

1. Cyclicity and naturalness: Working with lunar cycles connects the magician to natural cyclicity and strengthens sense of meaning.
2. Precision and expertise: Astrological knowledge creates a sense of precision and expertise, distinguishing the expert from the amateur.
3. Anticipation and tension: Waiting for the right time increases anticipation and intensifies emotional investment in the ritual.
4. Projection: The person projects their feelings onto the universe—the "dark moon" reflects their "dark feelings."

Anthropological Analysis

1. Cosmology of time: Every society develops a perception of sacred time—special times when supernatural power is stronger.
2. Synchronicity: Belief in connection between star positions and human events is universal.
3. Expertise and hierarchy: Astrological knowledge creates hierarchy—whoever can read the stars has special status.
4. Modernity and tradition: Even in the modern era, many people believe in the influence of stars and lunar cycles. This is a deep human pattern.

Pattern 20: Witchcraft Accusations

Definition

Finally, it is important to understand that much of what we know about black magic comes from accusations, not from documentation of actual practice. Witchcraft accusations are a complex social-psychological phenomenon.

Examples from Different Cultures

Medieval Europe: Witch hunts, witch trials, and executions. Hundreds of thousands of people, mostly women, were executed for witchcraft.

Modern Africa: Witchcraft accusations are still common, sometimes leading to violence, murder, and community expulsion.

Papua New Guinea: Witchcraft accusations lead to torture and murder, despite government efforts to eradicate the phenomenon.

Vanuatu: John's case demonstrates how witchcraft accusations reflect social tensions: return from the city, jealousy, land conflict.

United States: Even in the modern world, some people believe they are victims of witchcraft or "rootwork."

Psychological Analysis

1. Projection: Witchcraft accusations often reflect projection of forbidden feelings (envy, anger, desire) onto the other.
2. Explanation for suffering: Why did a child die? Why did crops fail? Why does illness strike? Accusing a witch provides a simple explanation.
3. Group cohesion: Accusing a witch unites the community against a common enemy, strengthening group boundaries.
4. Repression: The accusation represses internal conflicts within the community and directs them outward.

Anthropological Analysis

1. Social tensions: Witchcraft accusations often arise in times of change and tension—drought, war, economic change.
2. Gender and power: In most societies, the main accused are women, especially elderly women, widows, or women without male protection. This is patriarchal oppression.
3. Social function: Witchcraft accusations regulate behavior—whoever deviates from the norm risks accusation. This is a mechanism for maintaining social order.
4. Modernity and tradition: In the modern world, witchcraft accusations are perceived as "backward," but they continue to exist. This is a universal human phenomenon, not "primitive."
5. Medical anthropology: Wintrob demonstrates how understanding beliefs in witchcraft is essential for effective psychiatric treatment of patients who hold them.

Chapter 7: Summary and Conclusions

7.1 Universal Patterns

In this study, we have identified 20 universal patterns recurring in different cultures, without connection between them. These patterns include:

1. Law of Similarity: Use of a figure to represent a victim
2. Law of Contagion: Use of bodily substances for influence
3. Sacrifice: Giving something precious in exchange for divine favor
4. Communication with the dead: Appealing to the dead for knowledge and power
5. Distant cursing: Use of a bone or stick directed toward the victim
6. Written curses: Use of writing as a magical medium
7. Shapeshifting: Belief in witches changing form into animals
8. Poisons and plants: Use of poisonous and psychoactive plants
9. Child spirits: Worship of spirits of dead children
10. Blood sacrifice: Use of blood in rituals and pacts
11. Graves and cemeteries: Use of graveyard earth and bones of the dead
12. Crossroads: Rituals at crossroads as liminal places
13. Animals: Use of animals in curses
14. Sexuality and sexual ritual: Use of sexuality in rituals
15. Love curses: Magic to win love or bind partners
16. Protection and curse breaking: Magical protection systems
17. Elements: Use of fire, water, earth, and air
18. Death worship: Intentional work with death energies
19. Time and stars: Choosing appropriate time according to astral cycles
20. Witchcraft accusations: Accusing others as a social practice

7.2 Universal Explanations

Why do these patterns appear repeatedly? Several possible explanations:

1. Universal cognitive structures: The human brain is structured similarly throughout the world. Tendencies like associative thinking, seeking causality, and fear of the unknown are universal.
2. Universal human needs: The need for control, explanation, comfort, and justice—all are basic human needs. Black magic provides answers to these needs.
3. Universal social structures: Every society deals with conflicts, inequality, intergroup tensions, and the need to regulate behavior. Black magic serves as a mechanism for coping with these challenges.
4. Universal experience of death: Death is a universal experience, and every society develops ways to cope with it. Black magic is one of these ways.
5. Need for meaning: Humans need meaning. Black magic provides meaning to suffering and disaster—there is a reason, there is a culprit, there is a way to fix it.

7.3 Implications for Research and Society

Understanding the universal patterns of black magic is important for several reasons:

1. Mental health: Mental health professionals need to understand these beliefs to effectively treat patients who hold them.
2. Law and human rights: Witchcraft accusations still lead to violence and murder in many countries. Understanding the phenomenon is essential for prevention.
3. Intercultural dialogue: Recognition of the universality of these phenomena can promote intercultural dialogue and reduce racist stereotypes about "backwardness" or "primitiveness."
4. Study of religion: Black magic is part of the religious-magical continuum, and its study illuminates important aspects of religion and faith.
5. Study of psychology: The research illuminates basic psychological mechanisms like magical thinking, cognitive biases, and the nocebo effect.

7.4 A Final Word

This study is intended to illuminate a complex human phenomenon, not to encourage it. Black magic is a reality for millions of people worldwide—not as an actual supernatural force, but as a belief system that deeply affects their lives, fears, and behavior.

Understanding this phenomenon does not require belief in it. One can understand the psychology of a victim who believes they are under magical attack, without believing in the attack itself. One can understand the social role of witchcraft accusations, without believing in the reality of witchcraft.

As Nina Izel said: "I know healing exists because I experience it all the time. And I know black magic exists because we live in duality, and everything has its opposites. Where there is light, there is also darkness." Whether black magic is "real" objectively or not, it is subjectively real for those who believe in it, and its effects on their lives are entirely real.

Chapter 8: The Evolution of Behaviorism into Social Laws – The Psychological Connection Between Mass Behavioral Control and Black Magic

---

8.1 Introduction: Between Behavioral Psychology and Magic

Behaviorism and black magic share a surprising common ground: both deal with controlling and influencing human behavior through the manipulation of stimuli and responses. While behaviorism developed as a scientific discipline in the 20th century, the principles upon which it is based – classical conditioning, operant conditioning, and reinforcements – have existed in human intuition since time immemorial and are reflected in black magic rituals around the world.

B.F. Skinner, one of the fathers of behaviorism, argued that "human behavior can be shaped and controlled through consistent reinforcement systems." This claim surprisingly echoes ancient magical conceptions, according to which people can be controlled through rituals, curses, and amulets. Could it be that black magic is an ancient intuitive expression of behavioral principles that scientific psychology only discovered in the twentieth century?

This study examines the evolution of behavioral principles into magical "laws," and the way mass behavioral control is reflected in black magic rituals. It is argued that black magic is not merely a system of superstitious beliefs, but also an early socio-psychological technology for shaping behavior, utilizing the same mechanisms that scientific psychology later identified.

---

8.2 Foundations of Behaviorism: From Classical Conditioning to Social Reinforcements

8.2.1 Classical Conditioning

Ivan Pavlov demonstrated that a neutral stimulus can elicit a reflexive response if it is repeatedly presented together with an unconditioned stimulus. In black magic, this principle is expressed in creating associations between certain symbols and fear or punishment.

Magical example: The Australian Pointing Bone ritual [Ritual 75] creates powerful classical conditioning. The bone itself (originally a neutral stimulus) becomes, through shared belief and repeated rituals, a conditioned stimulus that evokes an extreme fear response. The victim, knowing the bone has been pointed at them, experiences real physical symptoms – a classic example of the nocebo effect, which is essentially reversed classical conditioning.

8.2.2 Operant Conditioning

Skinner showed that behavior is shaped through reinforcements (positive responses) and punishments (negative responses). In black magic, this system operates on two levels:

1. Shaping community behavior: The belief in the power of curses serves as social punishment. Whoever deviates from the norm risks being accused of witchcraft or cursed. Thus, magic becomes a behavioral enforcement mechanism.
2. Shaping the believer's behavior: Protection rituals, amulets, and cleansing baths [Pattern 16] provide positive reinforcements (feeling of security, reduced anxiety) for those who adhere to the required behavioral rules.

8.2.3 Social Learning Theory

Albert Bandura added that humans learn not only from personal experience but also by observing others (learning through imitation). In black magic, social learning is a central mechanism in the spread of beliefs and practices.

Magical example: The Thai Luk Thep phenomenon [Ritual 60] demonstrates how social learning spreads belief. When people see others treating dolls as real children and reporting success and luck, they imitate this behavior, and the belief strengthens within the community.

---

8.3 The Laws of Black Magic as Behavioral Principles

8.3.1 The Law of Similarity

The Law of Similarity states that "like affects like." This principle parallels the principle of classical conditioning, where a stimulus similar to the original stimulus evokes a similar response.

Behaviorist analysis: A Voodoo doll [Ritual 3] represents the victim. When the believer sticks a pin in the doll, they create an association between the harm to the doll and the expected harm to the victim. Conditioning is created through visual and symbolic similarity.

Behavioral implication: The Law of Similarity creates expectancy – a well-known psychological mechanism where expectation of a certain outcome increases the likelihood of its occurrence (placebo effect and its opposite).

8.3.2 The Law of Contagion

The Law of Contagion states that "things that have been in contact continue to influence each other." This principle parallels the principle of operant conditioning, where past outcomes influence future behavior.

Behaviorist analysis: Using the victim's hair, nails, or blood [Pattern 2] creates conditioning where the physical material (conditioned stimulus) evokes the same responses as the person themselves (unconditioned stimulus).

Behavioral implication: The Law of Contagion produces a feeling of "presence" and "connection" that strengthens the belief in the ability to influence, and consequently increases the psychological response.

8.3.3 Temporal and Astral Laws

The belief that time has a magical quality [Pattern 19] parallels the principle of reinforcement schedules in operant conditioning. Skinner showed that the timing of reinforcements affects the strength of learning.

Behaviorist analysis: Rituals performed during a black moon, at the hour of the bull, or on special astrological dates [Rituals 57, 97] create a unique time frame that increases anticipation and intensifies emotional investment. Waiting for the "right" time strengthens the psychological effect.

Behavioral implication: Magical schedules act as a "delayed reinforcement" mechanism – the waiting increases the value of the reinforcement and strengthens the conditioning.

8.4 Mass Behavioral Control: From Magic to Propaganda

8.4.1 Shared Control Mechanisms

Social psychologist Gustave Le Bon described in his book "The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind" (1895) how crowds operate according to a different logic than individuals. These mechanisms surprisingly parallel black magic rituals:

Psychological-Social Mechanism Expression in Political Propaganda Expression in Black Magic
Emotional Contagion Mass speeches, national symbols Mass night rituals, public curses [Ritual 6]
Group Identity "We" vs. "They" Witchcraft accusations as an internal enemy [Pattern 20]
Symbols and Rituals Flags, anthems Amulets, dolls, curse tablets [Pattern 6]
Symbolic Punishment Boycotts, exile Curses, social ostracism

8.4.2 Case Study: The European Witch Hunts

The witch hunts in Europe during the Middle Ages and Renaissance are a classic example of mass behavioral control through magical-psychological mechanisms.

Socio-behaviorist analysis:

1. Creation of a common enemy: The witch was defined as the "other" threatening the community. This created a cohesive group identity ("we" the good Christians vs. "they" the evil witches).
2. Collective conditioning: Through sermons, inquisition writings, and rumors, conditioning was created where certain signs (a mole, a black cat, unusual nighttime activity) evoked a collective fear response.
3. Reinforcements and punishment: The executions served as public punishment (negative reinforcement) deterring others from deviating from the norm. Simultaneously, participants in the hunt received positive reinforcements (feeling of security, social status).
4. Social learning: Witch trials were public events, and the audience learned by observing what constituted forbidden behavior and its punishments.

8.4.3 Case Study: The Santa Muerte Cult in Contemporary Mexico

The Santa Muerte cult [Pattern 18] in present-day Mexico demonstrates how black magic serves as a behavioral control mechanism among marginalized populations.

Socio-behaviorist analysis:

1. Immediate reinforcements: Drug dealers and criminals who turn to Santa Muerte receive a feeling of security and protection (positive reinforcement) immediately after the ritual.
2. Shaping behavior through fear: The belief that harming Santa Muerte devotees will bring a curse deters competitors and enemies (expected punishment).
3. Group cohesion: The cult creates a strong group identity among marginalized populations and reinforces in-group behaviors.

8.5 Black Magic Rituals as Social Enforcement Mechanisms

8.5.1 Public Curses

Public curses, like the Norse Niðing Pole [Ritual 6], serve as a means to enforce social norms through shame and humiliation.

Behaviorist analysis: The public curse acts as social punishment (negative reinforcement) deterring deviation from the norm. Knowing that certain actions will lead to a public curse shapes behavior through anticipation of punishment.

8.5.2 Witchcraft Accusations

Witchcraft accusations [Pattern 20] are a powerful mechanism for shaping social behavior.

Behaviorist analysis:

1. Indirect punishment: Accusation of black magic harms the social status of the accused, even without physical evidence. This is effective social punishment.
2. Reinforcing norms: Witchcraft accusations clearly define what constitutes forbidden behavior. Anyone who deviates from the norm risks accusation.
3. Group cohesion: The accusation unites the community against a common enemy and reinforces group boundaries.

8.5.3 Protection Rituals

Protection rituals [Pattern 16] act as an inverse behavioral control mechanism: they reinforce desired behavior by promising security and protection.

Behaviorist analysis: Protection rituals provide positive reinforcements (feeling of security, reduced anxiety) to those who adhere to the required behavioral rules. This is a "carrot" mechanism parallel to the "stick" mechanism of curses.

8.6 Linguistic Conditioning: The Power of Words in Magic and Propaganda

8.6.1 Words as Conditioned Stimuli

In behaviorism, words are conditioned stimuli that can evoke intense emotional responses. In black magic, words have real magical power.

Magical examples:

· Names of Hell [Ritual 15]: Calling demonic names creates a special state of consciousness and reinforces belief in their power.
· Finnish Curses [Ritual 21]: Spell songs (rune-singing) where the words themselves are believed to create reality.
· Incantations and Mantras: Repetition of certain words creates linguistic conditioning that reinforces belief.

Behaviorist analysis: Words gain emotional power through conditioning. When a particular word (such as a demon's name) appears repeatedly in threatening contexts, it becomes a conditioned stimulus that evokes a fear or awe response.

8.6.2 Written Language as a Tool of Control

Curse tablets [Pattern 6] demonstrate how written language serves as a behavioral control mechanism.

Behaviorist analysis: Writing creates "permanence" and "commitment" – written words are perceived as having greater validity than spoken words, and therefore influence expectations and responses more strongly.

8.8 Discussion: Why Do These Mechanisms Work?

8.8.1 The Universality of Human Conditioning

The behaviorist mechanisms identified in black magic are universal because they are based on fundamental properties of the human brain:

1. Neuroplasticity: The human brain responds to conditioning and repetitions by strengthening neural connections. Repeated magical rituals create neural wiring that reinforces belief.
2. Predictive Processing: The brain acts as a prediction machine, trying to anticipate the future based on the past. Magical rituals provide a sense of prediction and control.
3. Reward System: Dopamine release in response to positive reinforcements (feeling of security, success) reinforces magical behavior.

8.8.2 The Nocebo Effect as Negative Conditioning

The nocebo effect [2.2.6] is an extreme expression of the power of negative conditioning. When a person believes they are the victim of a curse, their expectation of harm creates real physical and mental symptoms. This is an example of conditioning where thought (conditioned stimulus) evokes a physiological response (unconditioned response).

8.8.3 The Role of Community in Reinforcing Conditioning

Social learning (Bandura) explains why magical beliefs are maintained across generations. When the entire community believes in the power of a curse, social pressure and collective fear amplify symptoms in the victim. This is a "collective nocebo effect" [Pattern 5] – powerful social conditioning.

8.9 Summary: From Magic to Science and Back

The behaviorist analysis of black magic reveals it to be a sophisticated socio-psychological system, based on the same principles that scientific psychology discovered centuries later.

Main conclusions:

1. Black magic as early behavioral technology: Black magic rituals apply principles of classical and operant conditioning, reinforcements and punishments, social learning, and behavior shaping.
2. Mass control through magic: Witch hunts, mass cults, and public curses serve as mechanisms for shaping social behavior and maintaining order.
3. Continuity between magic and propaganda: The same linguistic, symbolic, and ritual mechanisms operate in black magic and modern political propaganda.
4. Universality of mechanisms: The similarity between black magic rituals in isolated cultures stems from the universality of human conditioning mechanisms.
5. Placebo and nocebo effects: The power of belief in black magic is explained by the placebo and nocebo effects – expectation of an outcome creates a real outcome.

As Skinner argued, "human behavior can be shaped and controlled." Black magic is evidence that humans have always known this, intuitively, and used this knowledge to shape behavior – their own and that of others. The difference between black magic and scientific psychology is not in the principles, but in the language: what magic called "supernatural forces," psychology calls "conditioning," "reinforcement," and "social learning."
